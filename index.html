About Me

I love working with anything that dives, floats or flies!

I'm an Aerospace Engineering Ph.D. candidate at Virginia Tech, with a concurrent M.S. in Ocean Engineering — a combination that sounds odd until you look at what I work on. Air cushion vehicles, seagliders, and hydrofoils all live at the interface between air and water, allowing me to study the nuances of each fluid medium.

My research is on the nonlinear dynamics and stability of air cushion vehicles, in the VT iShip Lab and the Center for Marine Autonomy and Robotics (CMAR) labs at Virginia Tech. I am advised by Prof. Stefano Brizzolara and my work was funded by the Office of Naval Research. Day to day for me looked like Simulink models, Star-CCM+ runs, on field testing with a ROS data-acquisition system on a real craft, regularly telling me I was wrong about something.

What I've come to care about is the loop between the two: a model that can predict the vehicle dynamics and controls with the strong backing of test data. The work I want to keep doing is both — designing the tests and test benches that isolates the error, finding the parameters that modelling misses, and closing the gap between what we predict and what we actually see.

Before this I completed my M.S. at University of Michigan (Aerospace Egnieering) and a Mechanical engineering degree in Pune. I'm currently finishing my Ph.D. and looking for flight test, simulation, and vehicle-physics roles.

Dissertation

ONR · 5-DOF Dynamics ·Matlab/Simulink · Field test

Dynamics and Maneuvering of Small Hovercraft: A Validated Multi-DOF Model .

An air cushion vehicle on a slope is an underactuated, strongly nonlinear system. The skirt deforms, the cushion vents, the propeller controls surge and cushion pressure, and each of these have complex interactions. With only a rudder, the system remains underactuated and poses a non-linear behaviour with large drifts. The program asks a concrete question — can such a vehicle climb a 35° grade under control? — and my work is the whole chain from that question to a defensible answer.

  • Model. An integrated 5-DOF vehicle-dynamics model in Simulink/MATLAB coupling aerodynamic forces, propulsion airflow, and skirt–ground interaction. It's the tool behind every stability, control, and performance trade study on the program, and it predicts the vehicle's maneuverability rather than just its steady state.
  • CFD. External-flow analysis in Star-CCM+, end to end — geometry prep, meshing, solver setup, post-processing — to extract the aerodynamic parameters that feed the Simulink model instead of guessing at them.
  • Trade studies. Sensitivity studies relating skirt geometry, airflow rate, and trim to climb performance and stability margins. Results drove real subsystem design decisions and were presented at program design reviews to ONR.
  • Experiment. I direct the field testing: designed tests, sensor setups in C++ with an integrated an end-to-end data acquisition system in ROS, and ran static and dynamic tests to validate the theoretical model and calibrate its dynamic coefficients.

The interesting part is never the run that matches. It's the run that doesn't, and the weeks spent debugging whether the fault is in the skirt model, the testing conditions, or the sensor themselves. My motto with testing: What can go wrong, will go wrong. And so we extensively prepare test benches, both in lab and on field. Two papers have come out of this so far — the experimental validation work at OCEANS 2024 and the maneuvering and control simulations at SNAME FAST 2025. A journal paper for a 2-DOF model has been submitted.

Experience

Regent Craft · Summer 2025

Performance analysis for a full-scale seaglider.

A seaglider spends its life in three regimes — hull-borne, foil-borne, and flying in ground effect — so the usual performance metrics from either aircraft or boats only half apply. There was no established evaluation framework for the vehicle class, so much of the summer was deciding what "performance" even meant before measuring it.

  • Built the foundational Python performance-analysis codebase: ingest raw flight and sea-trial telemetry, compute aero/hydrodynamic coefficients, generate performance-envelope diagnostics.
  • Worked with the Hardware-in-the-Loop (HiL), test teams and the vehicle dynamics teams to study the test data and derive meaningful analysis.
  • Analyzed flight-test data, identified sources of model error, and updated the vehicle physics model.
  • Calibrated coupled aero/hydrodynamic physics-based models against Hardware-in-the-Loop and sea-trial data.
  • Delivered automated post-processing tools bridging HIL simulation output and real-world telemetry.

This is the project that convinced me I want to work where the simulation team and the test team argue with each other in person.
