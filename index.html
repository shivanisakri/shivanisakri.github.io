About

Where the air meets the water

I'm an Aerospace Engineering Ph.D. candidate at Virginia Tech, with a concurrent M.S. in Ocean Engineering — a combination that sounds odd until you look at what I work on. Air cushion vehicles, seagliders, and hydrofoils all live at the interface between air and water, and they punish you for treating either one as an afterthought.

My research is on the nonlinear dynamics and stability of single-propeller air cushion vehicles, in the VT iShip Lab at the Center for Marine Autonomy and Robotics, advised by Prof. Stefano Brizzolara and funded by the Office of Naval Research. Day to day that means Simulink models, Star-CCM+ runs, a ROS data-acquisition system on a prototype, and field tests that regularly tell me I was wrong about something.

What I've come to care about is the loop between the two: a model that never meets hardware is a hypothesis, and test data with no model behind it is just a spreadsheet. The work I want to keep doing is the part in the middle — designing the test that isolates the error, finding the parameter that was lying, and closing the gap.

Before this I did an M.S. at Michigan and a mechanical engineering degree in Pune. I'm currently finishing my Ph.D. and looking for flight test, simulation, and vehicle-physics roles.

Dissertation

ONR · 5-DOF Simulink · Star-CCM+ · field test

Nonlinear Dynamics and Stability of Single-Propeller Air Cushion Vehicles.

An air cushion vehicle on a slope is an underactuated, strongly nonlinear system. The skirt deforms, the cushion vents, the propeller wash shifts with trim, and each of those couples straight back into the others. Add a single propeller doing both lift and thrust and you lose the control authority that would let you sidestep the coupling. The program asks a concrete question — can such a vehicle climb a 35° grade under control? — and my work is the whole chain from that question to a defensible answer.

  • Model. An integrated 5-DOF vehicle-dynamics model in Simulink/MATLAB coupling aerodynamic forces, propulsion airflow, and skirt–ground interaction. It's the tool behind every stability, control, and performance trade study on the program, and it predicts the vehicle's maneuverability rather than just its steady state.
  • CFD. External-flow analysis in Star-CCM+, end to end — geometry prep, meshing, solver setup, post-processing — to extract the aerodynamic parameters that feed the Simulink model instead of guessing at them.
  • Trade studies. Sensitivity studies relating skirt geometry, airflow rate, and trim to climb performance and stability margins. Results drove real subsystem design decisions and were presented at program design reviews to ONR.
  • Experiment. I direct the field testing: designed and integrated an end-to-end data acquisition system in ROS, wrote the C++ that processes telemetry off it, and ran static and dynamic tests to validate the theoretical model and calibrate its dynamic coefficients.

The interesting part is never the run that matches. It's the run that doesn't, and the week spent working out whether the fault is in the skirt model, the inflow assumption, or the sensor. Two papers have come out of this so far — the experimental validation work at OCEANS 2024 and the maneuvering and control simulations at SNAME FAST 2025.

Experience

Regent Craft · Summer 2025

Performance analysis for a full-scale seaglider.

A seaglider spends its life in three regimes — hull-borne, foil-borne, and flying in ground effect — so the usual performance metrics from either aircraft or boats only half apply. There was no established evaluation framework for the vehicle class, so much of the summer was deciding what "performance" even meant before measuring it.

  • Built the foundational Python performance-analysis codebase: ingest raw flight and sea-trial telemetry, compute aero/hydrodynamic coefficients, generate performance-envelope diagnostics.
  • Analyzed flight-test data, identified sources of model error, and updated the vehicle physics model.
  • Calibrated coupled aero/hydrodynamic physics-based models against Hardware-in-the-Loop and sea-trial data.
  • Designed validation tests that isolate specific dynamic-model error modes, so a discrepancy points at one parameter instead of ten.
  • Delivered automated post-processing tools bridging HIL simulation output and real-world telemetry.

This is the project that convinced me I want to work where the simulation team and the test team argue with each other in person.
