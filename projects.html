Office of Naval Research · Dissertation program

Getting a hovercraft up a 35° hill

A hovercraft on a slope is an underactuated, strongly nonlinear system: the skirt deforms, the cushion vents, the propeller wash changes with trim, and every one of those couples back into the others. The program asks whether a single-propeller air cushion vehicle can climb a 35° grade under control — and my job is the whole chain from the question to the answer.

  • Model. An integrated 5-DOF vehicle-dynamics model in Simulink/MATLAB coupling aerodynamic forces, propulsion airflow, and skirt–ground interaction. It's the tool for every stability, control, and performance trade study on the program.
  • CFD. External-flow analysis in Star-CCM+ end to end — geometry prep, meshing, solver setup, post-processing — to extract the aerodynamic parameters that feed the Simulink model rather than guessing at them.
  • Trades. Sensitivity studies relating skirt geometry, airflow rate, and trim to climb performance and stability margins. Results drove real subsystem design decisions and were presented at program design reviews to ONR.
  • Test. Directed field testing. Designed and integrated an end-to-end data acquisition system in ROS, wrote the C++ that processes the telemetry off it, and calibrated dynamic coefficients to reconcile model against test.

The interesting part is never the run that matches. It's the run that doesn't, and the week spent working out whether the fault is in the skirt model, the inflow assumption, or the sensor.

Regent Craft · Summer 2025

Performance analysis for a full-scale seaglider

A seaglider spends its life in three regimes — hull-borne, foil-borne, and flying in ground effect — which means the usual performance metrics from either aircraft or boats only half apply. There was no established evaluation framework for the vehicle class, so a lot of the summer was deciding what "performance" even meant before measuring it.

  • Built the foundational Python performance-analysis codebase: ingest raw flight and sea-trial telemetry, compute aero/hydrodynamic coefficients, generate performance-envelope diagnostics.
  • Analyzed flight-test data, identified sources of model error, and updated the vehicle physics model — the same core loop that drives eVTOL and eSTOL flight-physics work.
  • Calibrated coupled aero/hydrodynamic physics-based models against Hardware-in-the-Loop and sea-trial data.
  • Designed and proposed validation tests that isolate specific dynamic-model error modes, so a discrepancy points at one parameter instead of ten.
  • Delivered automated post-processing tools bridging HIL simulation output and real-world telemetry.

This is the project that convinced me I want to work where the simulation team and the test team argue with each other in person.

Office of Naval Research · 2026 – present

Hydrofoil craft performance model

The newest project in the lab. I'm formulating a 2-DOF physics-based hydrodynamic performance model for a foil-borne craft and building a custom MATLAB simulator around it to study dynamics, control response, and stability.

  • Deriving the model from hydrodynamic first principles rather than fitting a black box, so the parameters stay physically interpretable when the test data disagrees.
  • Building the simulator as a reusable analysis tool for the program, not a one-off script.

Early days — this section will grow as results do.

2026 – present

Hoverski: turning a jetski into a hovercraft

A jetski already has a hull, a powerplant, and a person willing to sit on it. What it doesn't have is a cushion. This project adds one: a flexible skirt, a lift system, and a MATLAB/Simulink lift-and-cushion model to size both before anything gets cut.

  • Lift and cushion model in MATLAB/Simulink — cushion pressure, flow rate, and skirt behavior — used to size the lift system rather than guess it.
  • Skirt and lift system design, carried through to the physical build.
  • A deliberately hands-on complement to the analytical work: the fastest way to learn what a cushion really does is to build one and watch it misbehave.

Sinhgad Institute of Technology, SPPU · 2017–2018

Wall-climbing robot

An undergraduate project that turned into my first publication. The robot climbs rough walls and terrain for data collection, sampling, and surveillance — which mostly meant a long fight with suction adhesion on surfaces that refuse to be flat.

  • Led the design and fabrication of both the structure and the climbing mechanism.
  • Wrote the Arduino control algorithm regulating the suction pump for smooth vertical motion.
  • Ran a test campaign across different wall types to characterize where it held and where it didn't.
  • Published as Control of Dynamics and Development of a Wall Climbing Robot, Journal of Material Science and Mechanical Engineering, Vol. 5, Issue 2 (2018).

In hindsight it's the same problem I work on now: a flexible seal, a pressure difference, and a surface that won't cooperate.

Arizona State · Michigan · Virginia Tech · 2019–2023

Who stays in engineering, and why

A separate research thread, and one I'm not filing away quietly: several years of mixed-methods work on retention and equity in graduate engineering. Different data, same instinct — measure the system honestly, then say what the measurement means.

  • Arizona State (2020–21), with Prof. Jennifer Bekki. Qualitative and quantitative analysis of gender ratios in STEM and their effect on the retention of women doctoral students; built a conceptual framework for analyzing impostor syndrome in graduate women engineers, and traced statistically significant results back to common themes. Contributed to an ASEE paper on counseling-service use among doctoral women of color in STEM.
  • University of Michigan (2019–20), with Prof. Cindy Finelli. Studied student resistance to active learning and persistent misconceptions in undergraduate education; ran focus groups and did the qualitative analysis.
  • Virginia Tech Center for Equity (2023), with Dr. Holly Matusovich. Assessed organizational structures across engineering departments and consolidated a shared resource pool to inform more equitable graduate-education policy.
